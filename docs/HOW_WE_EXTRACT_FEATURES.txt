================================================================================
PARKINSON'S DISEASE PREDICTION SYSTEM
HOW WE EXTRACT FEATURES — IN PLAIN LANGUAGE
(Easy-to-understand version)
================================================================================

This document explains HOW we get the numbers (features) from your uploaded
video, image, or audio. It is written so that anyone can understand the
process without needing a technical background.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT THIS DOCUMENT COVERS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Part 1 — How we get 10 numbers from a WALKING VIDEO (gait)
  Part 2 — How we get 10 numbers from a HANDWRITING IMAGE
  Part 3 — How we get 22 numbers from VOICE / AUDIO
  Part 4 — What happens when you upload ONE VIDEO and ask for everything

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
BIG PICTURE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We never store your raw video or image for the prediction. We only turn it
  into a list of numbers (features). Those numbers describe things like: how
  regular the walking is, how smooth the handwriting is, how stable the voice
  is. The model then uses these numbers to give a result.

================================================================================
PART 1 — HOW WE EXTRACT GAIT FROM A WALKING VIDEO
================================================================================

  What you give us: A video of someone walking (e.g. toward the camera or
  across the room).

  What we give back: 10 numbers that describe how they walk (stride timing,
  speed, regularity, left–right balance).

  We do NOT use special equipment like force plates or motion-capture suits.
  We only use the video itself: we look at how the picture changes from one
  moment to the next and use that to estimate walking pattern.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 1 — Open the video and get ready
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We open the video file and read how many images per second it has (called
  “frame rate”). If the file doesn’t say, we assume 30 images per second. We
  only look at the first 300 images (about 10 seconds) so processing stays
  fast.

  In simple terms: We treat the video as a stack of still photos. We need
  to know how many photos per second so we can turn “image number” into
  “seconds” when we measure time.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 2 — See “how much moved” in each moment
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  For each image in the video we do this:

    1. Turn the image into black-and-white. Colour isn’t needed to see
       movement.

    2. Slightly blur the image. That hides tiny changes (like camera shake
       or skin detail) so we mainly see big movement (body, legs).

    3. Compare this image with the one before it. Where the picture changed
       a lot, we treat it as “moved.” We then count: what fraction of the
       image changed? That fraction (between 0 and 1) is our “motion amount”
       for that moment.

  We do this for every pair of images that follow each other. So we get a
  list of numbers: one number per moment, saying “how much of the scene
  moved” at that time.

  We also remember how bright each image is on average. Later, if the
  brightness jumps around a lot (e.g. camera moving or shaking), we can
  adjust our numbers a bit to reflect possible unsteadiness.

  In simple terms: Like putting two photos side by side and marking what
  changed. Lots of change = lots of motion (e.g. leg swinging). Little
  change = less motion (e.g. foot on ground). We turn that into one number
  per moment.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 3 — Find when each “step” happens
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Walking has a rhythm: step, step, step. In our list of “motion amount”
  numbers, that often shows up as peaks—moments when the motion is higher
  (e.g. when the foot swings or hits the ground).

  We smooth the list a little (mix each value with its neighbours) so tiny
  bumps don’t count as steps. Then we look for peaks: a moment that is
  higher than the moment before and the moment after, and also above a
  certain level. We also make sure we don’t count two peaks that are too
  close together (so one step isn’t counted twice).

  Result: we get a list of when we think a step happened (as image numbers;
  we can turn those into seconds using images per second).

  In simple terms: We find the “beats” of the walk. Each beat is one step.
  From that we can measure timing and rhythm.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 4 — Stride interval and its variation
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  One stride = one left step + one right step (one full cycle of both legs).
  The time for one stride is “stride interval.” We get the time between
  consecutive steps from our step list and how many images per second we
  have, then double it (two steps = one stride) and average. That gives
  stride_interval.

  How much that time varies from stride to stride (steady vs all over the
  place) gives us “stride_interval_std.” If the person walks very slowly
  (very little motion in the video), we nudge the stride interval a bit
  longer; if they walk fast (lots of motion), we nudge it a bit shorter.

  In simple terms: We measure how long each stride takes and whether that
  time is the same every time or changes a lot.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 5 — Swing time and stance time
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  When the foot is in the air we call that “swing.” When it’s on the ground
  we call that “stance.” We can’t see the foot directly in a simple video,
  so we use our motion numbers. We take the middle value of all motion
  amounts as a split point. Images with motion below that we treat as more
  “foot in the air”; images above as more “foot on ground.” We count how
  many images fall in each group, convert to time using images per second,
  and divide by the number of steps to get average swing time and stance
  time per step.

  In simple terms: We split the walk into “foot in the air” vs “foot on
  ground” using how much the image moves in each moment.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 6 — Double support time
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  “Double support” is the short time when both feet are on the ground
  (between steps). We estimate it from stance time and how much our motion
  numbers jump around. More jumpy motion can mean longer double support.

  In simple terms: We get a number that reflects how long both feet are on
  the ground at once.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 7 — Cadence and gait speed
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Cadence = steps per minute. We count how many steps we found in the
  video, divide by the length of the video in seconds, and multiply by 60.
  We then adjust a bit using how strong the motion was, and we keep the
  result in a realistic range (e.g. 70–150 steps per minute).

  Gait speed = how fast the person is moving (distance per second). We
  don’t know real distance from the video, so we guess a step length from
  how strong the motion is, then: (number of steps × guessed step length) ÷
  video length. That gives us a speed value we can use.

  In simple terms: We get “how many steps per minute” and “how fast they’re
  walking” from the step count and video length.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 8 — Step length (the feature)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  From speed and cadence we can work backwards to get an average step
  length: speed ÷ (steps per second). If we don’t have cadence, we use a
  simple guess from how strong the motion is.

  In simple terms: One number that represents how long each step is (on
  average), in distance.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 9 — Stride regularity
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We look at how consistent our “motion amount” numbers are. If they’re very
  similar from moment to moment, the walk is regular; if they jump around a
  lot, it’s irregular. We turn that into a single number between 0 and 1
  (higher = more regular). We keep it in a sensible range.

  In simple terms: One number that says how steady and even the walking
  pattern is.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 10 — Gait asymmetry
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We split our motion list into the first half of the video and the second
  half. We take the average motion in each half. If the two averages are
  different, the walk is somewhat “asymmetric” (e.g. one side or one part
  of the walk stronger than the other). We turn that difference into a
  number in a fixed range (e.g. 0.05–0.35).

  In simple terms: One number that says whether the walk is even on both
  sides or uneven.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 11 — Final tweaks from the video
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  If the overall motion in the video is very low, we slightly reduce speed,
  cadence, and regularity (so we don’t overestimate). If the brightness of
  the images changes a lot (possible shaking or unsteadiness), we slightly
  increase stride variation and asymmetry. Then we return all 10 numbers.

  In simple terms: We adjust the numbers a little so they better match what
  we actually see in the video.

PART 2 — HOW WE EXTRACT HANDWRITING FROM AN IMAGE
================================================================================

  What you give us: One image of handwriting or a drawing (e.g. a sentence,
  a spiral).

  What we give back: 10 numbers that describe the writing (pressure, speed
  estimate, tremor, fluency, etc.).

  We do NOT use a special pen or tablet that records pressure and time. We
  only have a single photo. So we estimate everything from the shape and
  thickness of the strokes in the image.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 1 — Load the image and separate writing from background
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We load the image and convert it to black and white. Then we choose a
  cutoff so that the written strokes become white and the rest (paper)
  becomes black. We use a method that picks the cutoff automatically from
  the image (so we don’t have to set it by hand). Now we have an image with
  only two colours: white for writing, black for background.

  In simple terms: We turn the photo into “writing = white, rest = black”
  so we can measure the writing alone.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 2 — Pressure (from stroke thickness)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We can’t measure real pen pressure from a photo, but thicker strokes
  usually mean more pressure. So we measure how thick the strokes are.

  For each point on a stroke, we find how far we are from the nearest edge
  of the stroke (how far “inside” the stroke we are). That distance (times
  two) is like the width of the stroke at that point. We collect these
  widths for the whole image. The average width (scaled to a number we use)
  becomes “mean pressure”; how much the width varies (thin in some places,
  thick in others) becomes “std pressure.”

  In simple terms: Thick strokes → higher pressure number; thin strokes →
  lower. We also measure how much the thickness changes from place to place.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 3 — Smoothness (and from it, velocity and acceleration)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Smooth, flowing writing tends to have gentle curves. Jerky or shaky writing
  has more sharp turns. We take the outline of the main stroke(s) and look
  at how much the direction changes from point to point. Lots of sharp turns
  = low smoothness; gentle curves = high smoothness.

  We then turn smoothness into estimates of “how fast” and “how smoothly
  the pen moved”: higher smoothness → higher mean velocity and mean
  acceleration; we also get a measure of how much that varies (std
  velocity). These are not real speed in cm per second, but numbers that
  the model can use.

  In simple terms: We look at how curvy or jagged the lines are. Smoother
  lines → we assume faster, more fluent writing and set the velocity and
  acceleration numbers higher.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 4 — Pen-up time (gaps between strokes)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  When you lift the pen, there’s a gap between strokes. We slightly thicken
  the strokes in the image so that strokes that are very close together
  join into one blob. Then we count how many separate blobs of writing there
  are. More blobs = more pen lifts = more time with the pen in the air. We
  turn that into a “pen-up” ratio and then into a “pen_up_time” feature.

  In simple terms: We count how many separate strokes there are. Many
  separate strokes → more time with the pen off the paper.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 5 — Stroke length and writing tempo
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We count how many dots (pixels) are part of the writing (total stroke
  length). We divide by 1000 to get a manageable number. For “writing
  tempo,” we use the opposite of pen-up: less pen-up → higher tempo (more
  continuous writing).

  In simple terms: One number is “how much writing” is on the page; the
  other is “how continuous” the writing is (tempo).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 6 — Tremor (wobble in the line)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Tremor often shows up as small wiggles in the stroke. We thin the stroke
  down to a single line right through the middle (like a backbone). Then
  we fit a straight line through that middle line. The real stroke wanders
  away from that straight line; we measure how much it wanders to the side.
  A wiggly stroke has high “wander”; a straight stroke has low. We turn that
  into a tremor score and then into a “tremor_frequency” number (in a range
  like 5–8, so the model can use it).

  In simple terms: We see how much the written line wobbles away from a
  straight line. More wobble → higher tremor number.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 7 — Fluency score
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We combine smoothness and tremor: high smoothness and low tremor → high
  fluency; low smoothness or high tremor → lower fluency. If the strokes
  are long and continuous (few, long outlines), we add a small bonus. We
  keep the result between 0 and 1.

  In simple terms: One overall number for “how smooth and fluent” the
  writing looks (0 = not fluent, 1 = very fluent).

  After this we return all 10 handwriting numbers.

PART 3 — HOW WE EXTRACT VOICE FROM AUDIO
================================================================================

  What you give us: An audio file (e.g. WAV, MP3) of someone speaking, or we
  take the sound track from a video.

  What we give back: 22 numbers that describe the voice (pitch, stability,
  noise, and some “pattern” measures).

  We use voice analysis software (Praat, via a library called Parselmouth)
  for most of the voice measurements. For a few extra numbers we use the
  pitch curve and simple maths.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 1 — Load the audio and prepare it
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We check that the file exists and is not empty or too big. We load the
  sound (the wave and how many measurements per second it has). We prefer a
  fast reader; if that fails we try others. We convert two-channel sound
  (left and right) into one channel and make sure the numbers are in a
  standard range. We also create a sound object that the voice software can
  use. If the software can’t read the file directly, we save the loaded
  audio to a temporary WAV file and use that.

  In simple terms: We get the audio into a form the analysis tools can use.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 2 — Pitch (how high or low the voice is)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We ask the voice software to find the “pitch” of the voice at each moment
  (how high or low it sounds). That gives us a curve of pitch over time. We
  ignore moments where there’s no clear pitch (e.g. silence or noise). From
  that curve we take:
    - the average pitch → “mean Fo” (MDVP:Fo(Hz))
    - the highest pitch → “Fhi”
    - the lowest pitch → “Flo”

  If something goes wrong we use safe default numbers so we don’t crash.

  In simple terms: We measure how high or low the voice is on average and
  its range (highest and lowest).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 3 — Jitter (how much the pitch jumps from moment to moment)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  The software can find the exact times of each “wave” of the voice (each
  vibration). Jitter is how much the length of those waves varies. We ask
  the software for several jitter measures (local, absolute, RAP, PPQ5, DDP).
  Each is a different way to express that variation. We store all of them as
  our jitter features (5 numbers). If the software fails we use small
  default values.

  In simple terms: Stable voice → low jitter. Shaky or unstable voice →
  higher jitter. We get several versions of this “shakiness” of pitch.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 4 — Shimmer (how much the loudness jumps from moment to moment)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Shimmer is like jitter but for loudness instead of pitch. We ask the
  software for several shimmer measures (local, in decibels, APQ3, APQ5,
  APQ11, DDA). We store all of them (6 numbers). Again, we use defaults if
  something fails.

  In simple terms: Even loudness → low shimmer. Uneven or trembling
  loudness → higher shimmer.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 5 — Noise vs tone (HNR and NHR)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  A clear voice has a strong “tonal” part (the clear, musical quality) and
  less noise. The software gives us a “Harmonics-to-Noise Ratio” (HNR):
  higher = clearer voice. We also compute a “Noise-to-Harmonics” style
  number (NHR) and scale it so it matches the range the model expects.

  In simple terms: One number says how “clean” the voice is (HNR); the other
  (NHR) is related to how much noise or breath there is.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Step 6 — Extra “pattern” measures from the pitch curve
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We take the pitch curve (the list of pitch values over time) and compute
  a few extra numbers that research uses for voice analysis:

    - RPDE: How “repetitive” vs “random” the pitch pattern is (we use a
      simplified version based on how the pitch changes from moment to
      moment).
    - DFA: How the pitch behaves over longer stretches (again simplified).
    - spread1 and spread2: How spread out the pitch values are (how much
      they vary around the average).
    - D2: A number that reflects how “complex” the pitch pattern is
      (simplified).
    - PPE: How “random” or “structured” the pitch sequence is (simplified).

  If we don’t have enough pitch data we use default values. Any invalid or
  infinite number we replace with 0 and log it.

  In simple terms: These six numbers describe the “shape” and “pattern” of
  the pitch over time in ways that can help the model.

  We then return all 22 voice features in a fixed order.

================================================================================
PART 4 — WHEN YOU UPLOAD ONE VIDEO AND ASK FOR EVERYTHING
================================================================================

  If you upload a single video and choose “extract voice,” “extract
  handwriting,” and/or “extract gait,” we do the following.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Voice from the video
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We use a tool that can take only the sound from the video and save it as
  a temporary WAV file (standard quality, one channel). Then we run the same
  voice extraction as in Part 3 on that WAV. When we’re done we delete the
  temporary file.

  In simple terms: We rip the sound from the video and analyse it like a
  normal audio file.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Handwriting from the video
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We open the video and take one image from the middle (so we’re more likely
  to get a useful picture). We save that image as a temporary file and run
  the same handwriting extraction as in Part 2 on it. Then we delete the
  temporary image.

  In simple terms: We take a snapshot from the middle of the video and
  analyse it as a handwriting image. Note: this only works if that image
  actually shows handwriting; otherwise the numbers are from whatever is
  in the frame.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Gait from the video
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We run the same gait extraction as in Part 1 on the full video (or the
  part we use). So the same video can give us gait and (if requested) voice
  and handwriting.

  In simple terms: We use the video as a walking video and get the 10 gait
  numbers from it.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What you get back
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  The system returns one response with up to three sets of numbers:
  voice_features (22), handwriting_features (10), and/or gait_features (10),
  depending on what you asked for. It also returns total_features (the
  count). No raw video or audio is stored for the prediction—only these
  numbers.

================================================================================
SUMMARY IN ONE PARAGRAPH
================================================================================

  For GAIT we treat the video as a sequence of images, measure “how much
  moved” between consecutive images, find the step rhythm from that, and
  from the rhythm and motion we compute 10 numbers (stride timing, speed,
  regularity, asymmetry). For HANDWRITING we turn the image into black-and-
  white strokes, measure thickness (pressure), smoothness and wobble (tremor),
  gaps (pen-up), and length, and combine them into 10 numbers. For VOICE we
  load the audio, use voice analysis software to get pitch, jitter, shimmer,
  and clarity (harmonics vs noise), and add simplified measures from the
  pitch curve to get 22 numbers. When you upload one video and ask for
  everything, we extract the sound and one middle image from the video and
  run voice and handwriting extraction on those, and gait on the video
  itself. All of this is done so that we only ever use lists of numbers for
  the prediction, not your raw files.
