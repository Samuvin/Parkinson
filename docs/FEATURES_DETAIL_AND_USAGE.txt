================================================================================
PARKINSON'S DISEASE PREDICTION SYSTEM
FEATURES — WHAT EACH ONE IS AND WHY WE USE IT
(Easy-to-understand version)
================================================================================

This file lists every number (feature) we get from your video, image, or
audio. For each one you will find:
  • What it is (in plain words)
  • How we get it (in plain words)
  • Why we use it (what it tells the model)
  • In simple terms (one-line takeaway)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  We take three kinds of input and turn them into numbers:

  Walking video  →  10 numbers (how the person walks)
  Handwriting    →  10 numbers (how the person writes)
  Voice / audio  →  22 numbers (how the person speaks)

  You need at least one kind of input. More kinds help the system be more
  accurate. All these numbers are sent to the model to get a result.

================================================================================
PART 1 — VIDEO GAIT FEATURES (10 numbers from walking video)
================================================================================

  We get these from a video of someone walking. We look at how the image
  changes over time and use that to estimate walking pattern.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. stride_interval
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How many seconds it takes to complete one full stride. One stride = one
    step with the left foot plus one step with the right foot (one full
    cycle of both legs).

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We find when each step happens in the video (from motion peaks). We
    measure the time between steps, double it (because two steps = one
    stride), and average.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    In Parkinson's, people often take longer for each stride (walk slower
    and more carefully). The model uses this number to spot stride times
    that are too long or too short.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How long one full left-right cycle of walking takes."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. stride_interval_std
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How much the stride time changes from one stride to the next. If every
    stride takes almost the same time, this number is low. If stride times
    jump around, this number is high (irregular rhythm).

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We look at the time between each pair of steps. We measure how much
    those times vary (standard deviation) and scale it.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    In Parkinson's, walking rhythm is often irregular (not steady). The
    model uses this to spot uneven walking patterns.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Whether the walking rhythm is steady or all over the place."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. swing_time
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How many seconds the foot is in the air during one step (not touching
    the ground).

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We split the video into moments with less motion (foot in air) and
    more motion (foot on ground). The less-motion time per step gives us
    swing time.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    In Parkinson's, the timing of "foot in air" vs "foot on ground" can
    change. This helps the model understand the walking pattern.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How long the foot stays in the air per step."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. stance_time
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How many seconds the foot is on the ground during one step.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    Same idea as swing time: we use motion in the video. Moments with more
    motion (foot on ground) give us stance time per step.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    In Parkinson's, people often keep the foot on the ground longer (more
    cautious, slower transition). With swing_time, this describes how the
    step is split between air and ground.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How long the foot is on the ground per step."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. double_support
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How many seconds both feet are on the ground at the same time (the
    brief overlap when you're moving from one foot to the other).

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We get it from stance time and how much the motion in the video varies.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    In Parkinson's, people often spend more time with both feet on the
    ground (for balance). The model uses this as a sign of stability and
    confidence while walking.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How long both feet are on the ground at once."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. gait_speed
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How fast the person is walking (distance per second). Simply: walking
    speed.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We count steps in the video and estimate how far each step is from the
    amount of movement. (Steps × step length) ÷ video length = speed.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Slower walking is a main sign of Parkinson's (slowness of movement).
    The model uses this as a strong clue for slowed movement.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How fast they are walking."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. cadence
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How many steps per minute. It's the rhythm or rate of stepping (e.g.
    100 steps per minute).

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We count how many steps happen in the video, divide by the duration
    in seconds, and multiply by 60. We keep the result in a normal range
    (e.g. 70–150 steps per minute).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    In Parkinson's, step rate is often lower. With speed and step length,
    this helps the model judge overall walking rhythm and slowness.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How many steps per minute."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8. step_length
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How far one step goes (distance covered in a single step), in metres
    or similar units. We estimate it from the video.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We get it from walking speed and cadence: speed ÷ (steps per second).
    If we don't have cadence, we use the strength of motion in the video.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Shorter steps (shuffling) are common in Parkinson's. The model uses
    this to spot a shortened step pattern.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How long each step is (distance)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
9. stride_regularity
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    A score (between 0 and 1) for how consistent the walking is. High
    score = very steady, even pattern. Low score = uneven, changing
    pattern.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We look at how consistent the "motion amount" is from moment to moment
    in the video. Steady motion → high regularity; jumpy motion → low.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Irregular, unsteady walking is a common sign of Parkinson's. This
    number tells the model how steady or variable the walk is.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How steady and even the walking pattern is (0–1)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
10. gait_asymmetry
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How different the left side is from the right (or the start of the
    video from the end). Higher number = more uneven (one side or one
    part stronger or different).

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We compare the average motion in the first half of the video to the
    second half. We turn that difference into a number in a set range.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    In Parkinson's, one side is often more affected, so the walk can be
    uneven. The model uses this to spot one-sided or unbalanced walking.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Whether the walk is even on both sides or uneven."

================================================================================
PART 2 — HANDWRITING FEATURES (10 numbers from a handwriting image)
================================================================================

  We get these from one photo of handwriting or a drawing. We don't use a
  special pen or tablet—only the image. So we estimate everything from
  how the strokes look (thickness, smoothness, wobbly lines, etc.).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. mean_pressure
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Average "pressure" when writing. We can't measure real pressure from
    a photo, so we use stroke thickness: thicker strokes = we assume
    higher pressure.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We measure how thick the pen strokes are at many points (how far from
    the edge of the stroke to the middle). We average those thicknesses
    and scale to a number we use.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    In Parkinson's, pressure can be weaker or more uneven (due to stiffness
    or tremor). The model uses this to understand force control while
    writing.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "On average, how hard the pen was pressed (from stroke thickness)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. std_pressure
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How much the pressure (stroke thickness) changes—some parts thin,
    some parts thick. High value = very uneven pressure.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We use the same thickness measurements as mean_pressure and measure
    how much they vary (standard deviation).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    A lot of variation in pressure can mean tremor or inconsistent control.
    The model uses it to spot irregular force while writing.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How much the stroke thickness varies (thin vs thick)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. mean_velocity
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Estimated "speed" of writing. We can't see real speed from one photo,
    so we guess from how smooth the lines are: smoother lines often mean
    faster, more fluent movement.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We look at the outline of the strokes and how much the direction
    changes (sharp turns vs smooth curves). Fewer sharp turns = higher
    smoothness = we set a higher "speed" number.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Slower writing is common in Parkinson's. The model uses this to spot
    slowness of movement in handwriting.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Estimated writing speed (from how smooth the lines are)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. std_velocity
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How much the estimated writing speed changes along the stroke—sometimes
    "faster," sometimes "slower" (from the shape of the line).

  HOW WE GET IT:
    From the same smoothness analysis we get a measure of how much that
    smoothness varies.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Uneven speed (stops and starts, hesitations) can point to motor
    problems. The model uses it to capture how fluent the movement is.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How consistent the writing speed is along the stroke."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. mean_acceleration
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Estimated "acceleration" of the pen—how much the movement changes
    speed. Smooth movement = more constant; jerky movement = changing
    acceleration.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We derive it from the same smoothness we use for velocity. Smoother
    strokes give a higher number.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Jerky or changing acceleration is linked to tremor and stiffness.
    The model uses it to capture how smooth the movement is.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Whether the pen movement is smooth or jerky."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. pen_up_time
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    How much time the pen is off the paper—i.e. gaps between strokes when
    the person lifts the pen. We give a number that acts like "proportion
    of time in the air."

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We slightly thicken the strokes in the image and count how many
    separate blobs of writing there are. More separate blobs = more pen
    lifts = higher pen-up. We turn that into a time-like number.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    In Parkinson's, people often pause more and lift the pen more (very
    small writing, hesitation). The model uses it to spot broken-up
    writing.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How much the pen was in the air (gaps between strokes)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. stroke_length
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Total amount of "ink" on the page—how much writing there is. We
    measure it as the number of stroke pixels (or a scaled value).

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We count all the pixels that are part of the writing (not background)
    and divide by 1000 to get a usable number.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Very little writing (small total length) can mean very small writing
    or less output. The model uses it as an overall measure of how much
    was written.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How much writing is on the page (total length of strokes)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8. writing_tempo
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    The overall "tempo" of writing—how continuous it is. High tempo =
    more continuous; low tempo = more stops and pen lifts.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We set it from pen-up: when pen-up is high, tempo is low; when pen-up
    is low, tempo is high (inverse relationship).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Slower or more interrupted tempo is common in Parkinson's. The model
    uses it to capture the rhythm of writing.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How continuous the writing is (few vs many pauses)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
9. tremor_frequency
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    A number (in Hz) that represents how much "wobble" or shake there is
    in the stroke—like tremor in the hand while writing.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We thin the stroke to a single line down the middle. We fit a
    straight line through it and see how much the real stroke wanders
    away from that line. More wander = more tremor; we scale it to a
    range around 5–8 Hz.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Tremor (at rest or while moving) is a core sign of Parkinson's. This
    helps the model detect tremor in handwriting even from one photo.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How much the line wobbles or shakes (tremor)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
10. fluency_score
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    A score from 0 to 1 for how smooth and flowing the writing is. 1 =
    very smooth and continuous; 0 = jerky, broken, or shaky.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We combine smoothness (from the shape of the strokes) and tremor.
    High smoothness and low tremor = high fluency. Long continuous
    strokes add a small bonus. We clip the result to 0–1.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Less fluent writing (jerky, hesitant, or shaky) is a key sign in
    Parkinson's. The model uses it as an overall measure of writing
    quality.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How smooth and flowing the writing is (0–1)."

PART 3 — VOICE / SPEECH FEATURES (22 numbers from audio)
================================================================================

  We get these from a recording of the person speaking. We use voice
  analysis software (Praat) for pitch, stability, and loudness, and
  simple maths on the pitch curve for a few extra measures.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. MDVP:Fo(Hz)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    The average pitch of the voice in Hertz (Hz). Pitch = how high or
    low the voice sounds. This is the average over the recording.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We use voice analysis software to get the pitch at each moment. We
    take the average of those pitch values (ignoring silence and noise).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    In Parkinson's, pitch can change (e.g. flatter, less variation). The
    model uses this as a basic measure of the voice.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Average pitch of the voice (how high or low it sounds)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. MDVP:Fhi(Hz)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    The highest pitch in the recording (in Hz). Together with the lowest,
    it shows the pitch range.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    From the same pitch curve we take the maximum pitch value.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    A narrow range (low highest pitch) can mean a flat or quiet voice.
    The model uses it to judge pitch variation.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Highest pitch in the recording."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. MDVP:Flo(Hz)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    The lowest pitch in the recording (in Hz). With Fo and Fhi, it defines
    the pitch range.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    From the same pitch curve we take the minimum pitch value.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    A narrow pitch range is common in Parkinson's speech. This helps the
    model see that.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Lowest pitch in the recording."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. MDVP:Jitter(%)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Jitter in percent. It measures how much the pitch "shakes" from one
    moment to the next—how stable the pitch is. High jitter = unstable
    pitch (shaky voice).

  HOW WE GET IT:
    The software finds each vibration cycle of the voice and measures how
    much the length of those cycles varies. We turn that into a percentage.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Higher jitter often means less stable voice (tremor, instability). It's
    a well-known marker in Parkinson's voice analysis.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How much the pitch shakes from moment to moment (%)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. MDVP:Jitter(Abs)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Same as jitter but in time (e.g. seconds) instead of percent. Another
    way to say "how unstable the pitch is."

  HOW WE GET IT:
    The same software gives us jitter in absolute time units.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Same as Jitter(%)—pitch instability—but in a different unit so the
    model can use both.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Pitch shakiness in time units (same idea as Jitter %)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. MDVP:RAP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    RAP = Relative Average Perturbation. Another measure of how much the
    pitch period (length of each vibration) varies—another form of jitter.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    The software computes it from the voice cycles over a short window.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    It gives the model another view of pitch irregularity (small changes
    from cycle to cycle).

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Another measure of pitch shakiness (jitter)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. MDVP:PPQ
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Jitter over a slightly longer window (5 cycles). So it's a "smoothed"
    jitter—one odd moment doesn't change it much.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    The software gives us this from the pitch periods (ppq5).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Smoothed jitter is more stable; the model uses it for a reliable
    measure of voice stability.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Pitch shakiness averaged over 5 cycles (smoother measure)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8. Jitter:DDP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Another jitter-related number—the average difference in jitter between
    consecutive periods. Often computed as 3 × RAP.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We get it from RAP (e.g. RAP × 3).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    It's part of the standard set of voice measures (e.g. UCI dataset)
    and helps the model judge voice quality.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Another pitch-perturbation measure (related to RAP)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
9. MDVP:Shimmer
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Shimmer = how much the loudness (amplitude) of the voice changes from
    one moment to the next. High shimmer = uneven, shaky loudness.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    The software measures the amplitude of each vibration cycle and how
    much it varies (shimmer).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Unstable loudness (e.g. weak or trembling voice) is common in
    Parkinson's. The model uses it for voice quality.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How much the loudness shakes from moment to moment."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
10. MDVP:Shimmer(dB)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Same as Shimmer but in decibels (dB)—the unit we use for loudness.
    Another way to express loudness variation.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    The software gives us shimmer in dB (local_dB).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Same idea as Shimmer—loudness instability—in dB for the model to use.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Loudness shakiness in decibels."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
11. Shimmer:APQ3
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Shimmer averaged over 3 cycles. A slightly smoothed measure of
    loudness variation so one odd moment has less effect.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    The software gives us this (apq3).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Helps the model judge how consistent the loudness is.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Loudness variation averaged over 3 cycles."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
12. Shimmer:APQ5
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Shimmer averaged over 5 cycles. Same idea as APQ3 but over a bit
    longer window.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    The software gives us this (apq5).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Another smoothed loudness measure that's standard in voice datasets.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Loudness variation averaged over 5 cycles."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
13. MDVP:APQ
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Shimmer over a longer window (11 cycles). So it's "long-term" loudness
    stability.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    The software gives us this (apq11).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Gives the model an overall picture of loudness stability over time.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Loudness variation over a longer stretch (11 cycles)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
14. Shimmer:DDA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Another shimmer-related number—the average difference in shimmer
    between consecutive periods. Often 3 × APQ3.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We get it from APQ3 (e.g. APQ3 × 3).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Complements the other shimmer numbers; part of the standard voice set.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Another loudness-perturbation measure (related to APQ3)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
15. NHR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Noise-to-Harmonics Ratio. How much of the voice is noise vs clear
    tone. Higher NHR = more noise, less clear voice (e.g. breathy).

  HOW WE GET IT:
    The software gives us a measure of how "tonal" the voice is
    (harmonicity). We turn that into a noise ratio and scale it.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Breathiness and noise are common in Parkinson's. NHR helps the model
    capture worsening voice quality.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How noisy or breathy the voice is (vs clear tone)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
16. HNR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Harmonics-to-Noise Ratio. How much the voice is clear tone vs noise.
    Higher HNR = clearer, more "musical" voice; lower = more noise.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    The software analyses the voice and gives us the mean harmonicity
    (HNR).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Lower HNR (more noise) is linked to voice problems in Parkinson's.
    The model uses it for overall voice quality.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How clear and tonal the voice is (vs noisy)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
17. RPDE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    A number that captures how "repetitive" vs "random" the pitch pattern
    is over time (complexity or regularity of the signal). The full name
    is Recurrence Period Density Entropy; we use a simplified version.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We look at how the pitch changes from moment to moment and compute a
    kind of "entropy" (randomness) from that.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Voice in Parkinson's can have a different pattern (more or less
    regular). RPDE helps capture that.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How regular or random the pitch pattern is over time."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
18. DFA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    A number that captures "long-range" structure in the pitch—how
    patterns repeat or scale over longer stretches. Full name: Detrended
    Fluctuation Analysis; we use a simplified version.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We analyse the pitch curve (how it goes up and down over time) with a
    simplified DFA method.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    That long-range structure can change in Parkinson's. The model uses
    it with other voice measures.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How the pitch pattern behaves over longer stretches."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
19. spread1
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    A measure of how "spread out" the pitch values are—how much they vary
    around the average. We store it in a compact form (using log).

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We take the pitch values, compute their average and how much they
    vary (standard deviation), and combine them (e.g. log of ratio).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Pitch spread helps the model capture how much the pitch varies in one
    number.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How much the pitch varies (spread of values)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
20. spread2
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Another measure of how spread out the pitch is (variation relative to
    the average). Similar to spread1 but a different formula.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We take the pitch curve and compute (standard deviation ÷ average).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Together with spread1, it gives the model a good picture of pitch
    variability.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "Another measure of pitch variation (spread)."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
21. D2
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    A number that reflects the "complexity" of the voice pattern—how
    complicated or structured the pitch behaviour is. The full name is
    correlation dimension; we use a simplified version.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We estimate it from the pitch series (how pitch changes over time).

  WHY WE USE IT:
    Voice dynamics can change in Parkinson's. D2 helps capture that
    complexity.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How complex or structured the pitch pattern is."

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
22. PPE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  WHAT IT IS:
    Pitch Period Entropy. How "random" or "structured" the sequence of
    pitch periods is. High entropy = more random; low = more regular.

  HOW WE GET IT:
    We build a histogram of the pitch periods (how often each value
    appears) and compute entropy from it, then scale it.

  WHY WE USE IT:
    That randomness can change in Parkinson's (more irregular or more
    rigid). The model uses it to describe the voice pattern.

  IN SIMPLE TERMS: "How random or regular the pitch sequence is (entropy)."

================================================================================
SUMMARY — HOW ALL FEATURES WORK TOGETHER
================================================================================

  • Every feature is just a number. The model scales these numbers and
    feeds them into a neural network (or a backup machine-learning
    model).

  • Gait (10 numbers): describe slowness, irregularity, asymmetry, and
    timing of walking.

  • Handwriting (10 numbers): describe pressure, speed, tremor, fluency,
    and pen lifts from one image.

  • Voice (22 numbers): describe pitch, pitch stability (jitter), loudness
    stability (shimmer), clarity (HNR/NHR), and pattern (RPDE, DFA,
    spread, D2, PPE).

  • The model combines all the numbers you send (from one or more types of
    input) and gives a prediction (e.g. Parkinson's vs Healthy) with a
    confidence score. This system is for research and screening only; it
    is not a substitute for a doctor's diagnosis.
